The Impact of Consumer Boycotts on Corporate Behavior

Consumer boycotts have long been a powerful tool for change. The Montgomery Bus Boycott of 1955-1956, sparked by Rosa Parks, led to the desegregation of public buses in Montgomery, Alabama, after a 381-day protest that cost the bus system significant revenue. More recently, the 2018 boycott of Nike by some consumers over their Colin Kaepernick campaign initially caused a dip in stock price, but Nike’s commitment to their values led to a 31% increase in sales afterward, showing how boycotts can push companies to double down on their principles—or change them.

Boycotts work by hitting companies where it hurts: their profits. When consumers collectively refuse to buy, companies are forced to listen. By boycotting Trump’s mega donors, we can pressure these corporations to reconsider their political affiliations and support more ethical causes. Your participation matters—every purchase you redirect to an ethical alternative sends a message!
